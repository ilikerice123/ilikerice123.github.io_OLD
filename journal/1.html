Trip to Toronto

Wow, it has been already more than a week since my last blog post on here, but to me it has only felt like a few days. I guess I have an excuse tho, since over thhe long weekend (civic holiday), my parents came to visit from toronto, and I trained over to meet them there.

We did the usual toronto things, like go to the CN tower, visit niagara falls, uot, browse uniqlo graphic tees (which were really nice by the way!) etc. The two days went by very quickly. Then, they drove all the way here to Ottawa to cook some meals for me and stay take a look around Ottawa for a day or two as well.

It went pretty much as expected - I had a lot of fun spending time with my brother again, and being with my family was also really enjoyable. However, one thing that really stuck out to me that I really didn't consider was how much I missed my parents. How much my parents' and my brother's unconditional love really meant to me. Sure, we had a few arguments over where we should park in downtown Toronto (traffic really is even a bigger mess there than in vancouver) and also about whether or not marijuana should be legalized (I really think that if alcohol and cigarettes are already legal, marijuana should fall under the same umbrella), but for the most part, I be as genuine to my true self as physically possible.

I consider myself to be a pretty introverted guy (I guess I'm not really sure if I'm just introverted, or just really shy, or maybe both), but I do feel like I could keep up a conversation if I was forced to, BUT BY BEING INOFFENSIVE. I think that's my biggest problem. When I talk to others, I'm so busy obssessing over how other people will feel about what I say that I often just take the inoffensive route and not say anything at all. This is not to say that I DON'T open up to people at all, but I do feel like it's a major roadblock when it comes to meeting new people (which I'm doing on coop!), and is pretty exhausting. However, in front of my parents, I genuinely feel so at home, and so unconditionally loved that I don't to put up any "walls" so to speak.

And I'm really going to miss that feeling of being completely free and surrounded by people that love me for the next four months. Being at home alone, cooking your own meals, and being self-sufficient with everyone around you expecting you to be excelling at your coop (which, might I add, technically speaking, I'd say I'm doing pretty good ðŸ˜‰) really takes its toll on me personally.

I guess this is sort of a depressing blog entry, but hopefully in the future, I can come back to this, look at it, and smile, shaking my head at how naive I am now. But I do feel like what I'm writing does hold a grain of truth for me in my current state. Perhaps it is also due to other issues that I won't go into right now that's causing me to write in such a pessimistic fashion. O well, you know what they say, life's a rollercoaster!

Reviving an Old Project and the Perils of a Software Engineer

So last year, in an attempt to improve my net-worth to potential employers I fiddled around to make an android app to convince not only coop companies but also myself that I'm at least a somewhat competent software engineer. My app was called "Track My Time", and basically what it would do is you would add places that you would like to track, and using gps locations on your phone, it would try and track how long you spend in each location.

This app actually worked quite well - it did what it was supposed to do, to my surprise, and was probably only off by only a few minutes after a week of attending lectures. However, for some weird reason, it broke, maybe about a month into "finishing" it (cuz projects are never finished ðŸ˜¤), and since I had already found a coop and school was getting busy with project deadlines coming up, I prompty forgot about it...until 3 days ago.

I was on the train with a bunch of coworkers and I was showing them how my app was supposed to work, and I was like "hey, for fun, I'm going to press the start tracking locations button. in the past this would have started the parselocationservice and started tracking your data!". Back then, I was stupid and didn't know how to use the logging feature (log.cat) on android, so I toasted everything (LOL), which resulted in huge toasts filling up my entire screen, and to my big surprise, these annoying toasts kept popping up! It was like a miracle!

The problem with my app before was that whenever someone swiped away the app, it would just force-close, and the service wouldn't persist, but for some peculiar reason, that wasn't the case this time. So, now 3 days later, with some spare time on my hands before I sleep, I decided to boot into linux and open up my app and see if I can figure out what was wrong.

Lemme just start off saying that, holy crap, me from one year ago was a fricken trooper. For the past 3 months, I've been writing code on a 3 monitor setup, 2 high resolution big monitors and one laptop screen. However, my laptop at home is a mediocre 14in 1600x900 LOOOL. I have ABSOLUTELY no clue how I developed ANYTHING on this without pulling my hair out or developing some rare eye disease. I guess ignorance is bliss... now how am I supposed to go back to school now with only my work laptop after my coop term ends??

Picture for reference:

The second thing I noticed was the ABSOLUTE mess that was my code. I have comments thrown in haphazardly, some of them witty, some of them useful, but the majority of them were written without any forethought on how someone reading them in the future with no context of the situation might interpret them. There were many foreign methods and concepts cooked into my code that I had no idea where to start. The funny thing is, I distinctly remember younger me thinking "o man, look at these comments that I'm writing, I'll be able to go back to this code at any time and just pick up where I left off!". Honestly, how I felt looking at my code at that moment was not that dissimilar to how a layman, after finding that their car broke down in the middle of the road, would feel when they open their car hood and a hiss of steam rises out of it. Just like the layman, I have a general idea of what all my classes do, but no specific details on how it does it.

Well after looking around and poking at the jumbled jenga that is my code, I realized that I had two classes doing the same thing. I had distinctly remembered one of these was deprecated, and I thought for sure I put a comment at the top saying which one was, but I could find no such thing. After tracing through my code, I realized that the service it was calling in fact didn't have any method to handle the location that was being passed into it. I checked the other class and it did, so guess what I did, I copied it over from the other class and woop-d-doo it seems my app has spluttered to a start again and is now noisily idling at the side of the road, waiting for me to drive it until it breaks again. :D

I guess the main takeaway for this is that not only should I write CLEAN code, and comment regularly, I have to write THOUGHTFUL and CONCISE comments as well. Nobody (including myself who wrote the code even) wants to slog through paragraphs of incoherent commented text to hear the ramblings of a sleep-deprived cpen student. Comments in source code should be for the code itself. Save everything else for blogs (like this!).

As an afterthought: When opened my app, I noticed that in my android manifest file, since I declared my app for a certain version of android api, a lot of the things were deprecated, or the ide encouraged me to update my classes and such to use the latest features. This got me thinking: even though my app could be in a finished and working state now, if I don't keep up with the constant updates and upgrades that android receives, my app is going to be dead in maybe as little as a year. Thus is the life of a software engineer designing solutions to problems on a continously evolving platform.
